1. Why

We can give the set of bounded linear functions between two norm spaces a norm.

2. Definition

The norm of a bounded linear function is the smallest real number by which we can bound the result on a vector times the norm of that vector.

2.1 Notation

Let $((V_1, F_1), \norm{\cdot}_1)$ and $((V_2, F_2), \norm{\cdot}_2)$ be two norm spaces. Let $f: V_1 \to V_2$ be linear and bounded. The norm of $f$ is the smallest $C$ so that \[ \norm{f(v)}_2 \leq C\norm{v}_1. \]

3. Equivalent Formulation

\begin{prop} Let $((V_1, F_1), \norm{\cdot}_1)$ and $((V_2, F_2), \norm{\cdot}_2)$ be two norm spaces. Let $f: V_1 \to V_2$ bounded and linear. The norm of $f$ is \[ \sup_{\norm{x}_1 = 1} \norm{f(x)}_2. \] \end{prop}
