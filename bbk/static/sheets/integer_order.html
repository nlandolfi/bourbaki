1. Why

We want to order the integers.

2. Definition

Consider $\eqc{(a, b)}, \eqc{(b, c)} \in \Z$. If $a + d < b + c$, then we say that $\eqc{(a, b)}$ is \t{less than} $\eqc{(b, c)}$.\footnote{One needs to show that this is well-defined. The account will appear in future editions.} If $\eqc{(a, b)}$ is less than $\eqc{(b, c)}$ or equal, then we say that $\eqc{(a, b)}$ is \t{less than or equal to} $\eqc{(b, c)}$.

3. Notation

If $x, y \in \Z$ and $x$ is less than $y$, then we write $x < y$. If $x$ is less than or equal to $y$, we write $x \leq y$.

4. Positive and Negative Integers

We call an integer $z$ \t{positive} if $z > 0$ and we call $z$ \t{negative} if $z < 0$.\footnote{Some authors use the term positive for the case when $z > 0$ or $z = 0$. We use the term nonnegative in this case.} We call an integer $z$ \t{nonnegative} if $z > 0$ or $z = 0$ and \t{nonpositive} if $z < 0$ or $z = 0$.

4.1 Notation

We denote the set $\Set{z \in \Z}{z \geq 0_{Z}}$ by $\Z_{++}$.
