1. Why

We want to generalize the notion of continuity.

2. Definition

A topological space is a subset system for which: (1) the \rt{empty set}{emptyset} and the \rt{base set}{baseset} are \rt{distinguished}{distinguishedsubset}, (2) the intersection of a finite family of \rt{distinguished subsets}{distinguishedsubsets} is distinguished, and (3) the union of a family of \rt{distinguished subsets}{distinguishedsubsets} is distinguished. We call the set of \rt{distinguished subsets}{distinguishedsubsets} the topology. We call the \rt{distinguished subsets}{distinguishedsubsets} the open sets.

2.1 Notation

Let $A$ be a non-empty set. For the set of distinguished sets, we use $\mathcal{T}$, a mnemonic for topology, read aloud as "script T". We denote elements of $\mathcal{T}$ by $O$, a mnemonic for open. We denote the topological space with base set $A$ and topology $\mathcal{T}$ by $(A, \mathcal{T})$. We denote the properties satisfied by elements of $\mathcal{T}$: \begin{enumerate}

\item $X,\emptyset \in \mathcal{T}$

\item $\set{O_i}_{i = 1}^{n} \subset \mathcal{T} \implies \intersect_{i = 1}^{n} O_i \in \mathcal{T}$

\item $\set{O_\alpha}_{\alpha \in I} \subset \mathcal{T} \implies \union_{\alpha \in I} \in \mathcal{T}$

\end{enumerate}
