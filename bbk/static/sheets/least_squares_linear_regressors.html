1. Why

What is the best linear regressor if we choose according to a squared loss function.

2. Definition

Let $X \in \R^{n \times d}$ and $y \in \R^d$. In other words, we have a paired dataset of records with inputs in $\R^d$ (the rows of $X$) and outputs in $\R$ (the elements of $y$).

A \t{least squares linear predictor} or \t{linear least squares predictor} is a linear transformation $f: \R^d \to \R$ (the field is $\R$) which minimizes \[ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^{n} (f(x^i) - y_i)^2. \] over the dataset of pairs $(x^1, y_1), \dots, (x^n, y_n) \in \R^d \times \R$ where $\transposep{x^i}$ is the $i$th row of $X$ for $i = 1, \dots, n$.

The set of linear functions from $\R^d$ to $\R$ is in one-to-one correspondence with $\R^d$. So we want to find $\theta \in \R^d$ to minimize \[ \frac{1}{n} \normm{X\theta - y}^2. \]

3. Solution

\begin{proposition} There exists a unique linear least squares predictor and its parameters are given by $\invp{\transpose{X}X}\transpose{X}y$.\footnote{Future editions will include an account.} \end{proposition}
