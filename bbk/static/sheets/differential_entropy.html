1. Why

We want to extend our notion of entropy (see \sheetref{discrete_entropy}{Discrete Entropy}) to real-valued (continuous) random variables.

2. Definition

The \t{differential entropy} of a probability density function is the integral of the density against the negative log of the density. This definition made to be similar to the case of discrete entropy. If a real-valued random variable has a density, then we call the differential entropy of its density the \t{differential entropy} of the random variable.

2.1 Notation

Let $f: \R^n \to \R$ be a probability density function. The differential entropy of $f$ is \[ - \int f \log f \] We denote the differential entropy of $f$ by $h(f)$.

3. Example

Let $x: \Omega \to \R$ be uniform on $[0, \nicefrac{1}{2}]$. Then $h(x) = \log\nicefrac{1}{2} < 0$.

4. Problems

We have $h(ax) = h(x) + \log\abs{a}$. In generaly $h(Ax) = h(x) + \log\abs{A}$.

5. Differences still meaningful

Even though the value of the differential entropy is not necessarily a good analogy to discrete entropy, differences still are. In particular, the following holds \[ I(X; Y) = H(Y) - H(Y \mid X) = H(X) = H(X \mid Y) \]
