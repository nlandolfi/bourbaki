\section{Definition}

Define $S \in \R^{d \times d}$ by

\[ S = \bmat{ 0 & 0 & \dots & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \ddots & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 & 0 } \] For a vector $x \in \R^d$ the \t{down shift} of $x$ is $Sx$.

Let $A \in \R^{d \times d}$ be a matrix with columsn $a_1, \dots, a_d$. $A$ is a \t{circulant matrix} if $a_1 = Sa_d$, $a2 = Sa_2$, and $a_i = A_{i -1}$ for $i = 2, \dots, d$.

1.1 Example

For example, the matrix \[ \bmat{ 1 & 4 & 3 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 & 4 & 3 \\ 3 & 2 & 1 & 4 \\ 4 & 3 & 2 & 1 \\ } \] is a circulant matrix.

2. Characterization

A matrix $C \in \R^{d \times d}$ is circulant if and only if there exists $c_0, \dots, c_{d-1}$ so that \[ C = c_0I + c_1 S + c_2S^2 + \cdots + c_{n-1}S^{n-1}. \]

3. Properties

The sum and product of any two circulant matrix is circulant. In other words, the circulant matrices with the usual matrix addition and multiplication form a commutative ring.
