1. Why

We want to find roots of negative numbers

2. Definition

A \t{complex number} is an ordered pair of real numbers. The \t{real part} of a complex number is its first coordinate. The \t{imaginary part} of a complex number is its second coordinate.

The \t{complex conjugate} (or just \t{conjugate}) of a complex number $z$ is the complex number whose real part matches $z$ and whose imaginary part is the additive inverse of $z$. The complex conjugate of a real number (imaginary part is zero) is the real number. In other words, the complex conjugate of a complex number with no imaginary part is the same complex number.

2.1 Notation

Let $z$ be a complex number. We denote the real part of $z$ by $\re(z)$, read "real of z,} and the imaginary part by $\im(z)$, read \say{imaginary of z." If $z = (a, b)$, then $\re(z) = a$ and $\im(z) = b$.

We denote the complex conjugate of the complex number $z \in \C$ by $\Cconj{z} \in \C$. Another common notation, not used in these sheets is $\overline{z}$ or $\bar{z}$. If there exists $a, b \in \R$ so that $z = (a, b)$, then $\Cconj{z} = (a, -b)$.
