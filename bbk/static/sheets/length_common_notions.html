1. Why

We want to define the length of a subset of real numbers.

2. Notions

We take two common notions:

\begin{enumerate}

\item The length of a whole is the sum of the lengths of its parts; the \t{additivity principle}.

\item The length of a whole is the at least the length of any whole it contains the \t{containment principle}.

\end{enumerate}

The task is to make precise the use of "whole,}, \say{parts," and "contains." We start with intervals.

3. Definition

By whole we mean set. By part we mean an element of a partition; in other words, a subset. By contains we mean set inclusion.

The length of an interval is the difference of its endpoints: the larger minus the smaller (see \sheetref{interval_length}{Interval Length}). Two intervals are \t{non-overlapping} if their intersection is a single point or empty. The \ct{length} of the union of two non-overlapping intervals is the sum of their lengths.
