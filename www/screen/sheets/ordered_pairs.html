Ordered Pairs

Why

We speak of an ordered pair of objects: one selected from a first set and one selected from a second set.

Definition

Let $A$ and $B$ be non-empty \rt{sets}{set}. Let $a \in A$ and $b \in B$. The ordered pair of $a$ and $b$ is the set $\set{\set{a}, \set{a, b}}$. The first element of $\set{\set{a}, \set{a, b}}$ is $a$ and the second element is $b$.

The cartesian product of $A$ and $B$ is the set of all ordered pairs. If $A \neq B$, the ordering causes the cartesian product of $A$ and $B$ to differ from the cartesian product of $B$ with $A$. If $A = B$, however, the symmetry holds.

Notation

We denote the ordered pair $\set{\set{a},\set{a,b}}$ by $(a, b)$. We denote the cartesian product of $A$ with $B$ by $A \cross B$, read aloud as "A cross B." In this notation, if $A \neq B$, then $A \cross B \neq B \cross A$.
